# Alpine-Style Cheese

**Purpose:** Story guide for Alpine-style cheese (Gruyère, Emmental, Comté family, plus Vermont versions like Spring Brook *Tarentaise*, Jasper Hill *Whitney*, Parish Hill *Idyll*).

## Why this style matters

Alpine-style cheeses are associated with mountain cheesemaking traditions in places such as Switzerland, France, Austria, Italy, and neighboring regions. The category includes reference cheeses such as Gruyère, Emmental, Comté, Appenzeller, Beaufort, Fontina, and many American adaptations.

The style is not one cheese. It is a family of solutions.

Historically, large wheels of cooked, pressed cheese helped transform seasonal mountain milk into a durable, transportable, valuable food. Communal or cooperative cheesemaking often mattered because one farm’s milk alone might not be enough for large-format cheese.

## Common characteristics

Possible traits include:

- cow’s milk,
- cooked curd,
- pressed wheels,
- medium to long aging,
- nutty, brothy, fruity, grassy, or savory flavors,
- smooth to firm texture,
- rind management, often including washing or brushing,
- strong relationship between milk quality and aging potential.

## Story possibilities

An Alpine-style story may involve:

- turning seasonal milk into durable value,
- adapting mountain methods to Vermont land and climate,
- cooperation among farmers, makers, and affineurs,
- the relationship between pasture and flavor,
- technical judgment in cooking, pressing, salting, and aging,
- a cheese tradition built around patience and transformation.

## Questions to ask the maker

- What part of the Alpine tradition are you preserving?
- What part are you changing?
- Why does this style make sense in your place?
- Is the cheese made in a large format? Why or why not?
- What role does aging play?
- What would a cheesemonger compare it to?
- What does a consumer notice first?

## Pitfalls

Do not imply the cheese has the same history as European Alpine cheeses unless the maker says so.

Do not make “Alpine” mean merely “nutty and firm.”

Do not force a mountain story if the stronger story is technical, local, economic, or sensory.
