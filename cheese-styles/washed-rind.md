# Washed-Rind Cheese

**Purpose:** Story guide for washed-rind cheese — daily attention, microbial life as craft partner, the aroma-versus-flavor surprise.

## Why this style matters

Washed-rind cheeses are ripened through active surface care. Makers wash, brush, smear, or otherwise tend the rind, often encouraging bacteria and yeasts that create strong aromas, savory flavors, and distinctive rind development.

The style is one of the clearest examples of cheese as a living process.

## Common characteristics

Possible traits include:

- repeated rind washing or brushing,
- orange, pink, brown, sticky, or tacky rinds,
- savory, meaty, brothy, earthy, or pungent aromas,
- soft to semi-soft paste,
- strong contrast between smell and flavor,
- close relationship between aging environment and final cheese.

## Story possibilities

A washed-rind story may involve:

- daily attention and risk,
- the maker actively shaping a living rind,
- aroma as invitation rather than defect,
- microbial life as craft partner,
- the difference between what a cheese smells like and what it tastes like,
- a cheese that rewards curiosity.

## Questions to ask the maker

- What do you wash it with?
- How often do you wash it?
- What are you looking for on the rind?
- What can go wrong?
- How do you explain the aroma to a hesitant customer?
- What should someone notice when tasting it?

## Pitfalls

Do not equate washed rind only with “stinky.”

Do not scare off consumers with technical microbiology unless the audience wants it.

Do not pretend every washed-rind cheese is extreme. Some are gentle, sweet, or balanced.
