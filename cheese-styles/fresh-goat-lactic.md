# Fresh Goat and Lactic-Set Cheese

**Purpose:** Story guide for fresh goat and lactic-set cheeses. Restore range and specificity beyond "tangy white logs."

## Why this style matters

Fresh goat and lactic-set cheeses can look simple, but they often reveal subtle milk, acidity, texture, and seasonality.

Because many consumers think “goat cheese” means one thing — tangy white logs — the story often needs to restore range and specificity.

## Common characteristics

Possible traits include:

- goat milk or mixed milk,
- lactic coagulation,
- fresh, ash-ripened, bloomy, or lightly aged formats,
- bright acidity,
- delicate texture,
- citrus, grass, cream, mineral, floral, or earthy notes,
- sensitivity to season and milk quality.

## Story possibilities

A fresh goat story may involve:

- freshness and immediacy,
- the personality of goat milk,
- the precision required to make simple cheese well,
- a farm’s herd and seasonal milk,
- the difference between mass-market goat cheese and farmstead or artisan versions.

## Questions to ask the maker

- What do customers assume when they hear “goat cheese”?
- How is yours different from the supermarket version?
- What is the texture supposed to be?
- Is acidity central or subtle?
- Is this cheese eaten fresh, ripened, ash-coated, or aged?
- What changes through the season?

## Pitfalls

Do not rely on “tangy.”

Do not assume goat cheese is rustic, funky, or sharp.

Do not ignore delicacy. Sometimes the story is about restraint.
