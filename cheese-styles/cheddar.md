# Cheddar

**Purpose:** Story guide for cheddar — the most commodity-vulnerable category and the one whose story most depends on restoring specificity.

## Why this style matters

Cheddar is an old English cow’s-milk cheese associated with the village and region of Cheddar in Somerset. It became one of the world’s most widely made and recognized cheese styles.

Cheddar can be farmhouse, clothbound, block, fresh, aged, sharp, mild, industrial, or deeply artisanal. Because the word is so familiar, it is especially vulnerable to commoditization.

## Core story challenge

If a maker says “cheddar,” many customers think they already know what it is.

The story must restore specificity.

A cheddar story may need to explain:

- the milk,
- the cheddaring process,
- clothbound or rind approach,
- aging time,
- texture,
- sharpness,
- farmstead or creamery context,
- what distinguishes this cheddar from commodity cheddar.

## Story possibilities

A cheddar story may involve:

- reclaiming a familiar cheese from industrial sameness,
- showing that “cheddar” is a method and tradition, not just an orange block,
- the transformation of curd through cutting, stacking, milling, salting, pressing, and aging,
- a regional version of a familiar style,
- the patience and risk of long aging.

## Questions to ask the maker

- What kind of cheddar is this?
- Is it clothbound, block, bandaged, waxed, rind-aged, or another format?
- What does aging do to it?
- What do people expect when they hear “cheddar,” and how does yours differ?
- Is sharpness the main point, or something else?
- What is most misunderstood about this cheese?

## Pitfalls

Do not assume cheddar must be orange, sharp, crumbly, or industrial.

Do not describe a cheddar only as “sharp and creamy” unless there is nothing better.

Avoid language that could describe any grocery-store cheddar.
