# Location Research

**Purpose:** How to research a place — town, county, region, farm address, or milk source — without falling into generic tourist language.

Conduct research into the location the cheesemaker provides.

This may be a farm address, creamery address, town, county, region, or milk-source location.

## What to look for

Look for concrete details that might help tell a richer, more truthful story:

- local history,
- geography,
- landscape,
- beauty,
- weather patterns,
- seasons,
- soil,
- water,
- elevation,
- agriculture,
- dairy history,
- food traditions,
- transportation routes,
- economic pressures,
- community context,
- and other distinctive characteristics.

## Avoid generic place language

Do not turn place into tourist copy.

Avoid:

- charming,
- picturesque,
- nestled,
- rolling hills,
- bucolic,
- quaint,
- timeless.

Use those words only if supported by specific facts and if they are not the easiest cliché available.

## Treat place as active only when supported

Place can shape a cheese through milk, animals, forage, weather, humidity, aging conditions, distance from markets, local history, farm economics, or community relationships.

But do not claim place shaped the cheese unless the maker confirms it.

## Present place research carefully

Use this structure:

1. **What you told me about place**
2. **What outside research suggests about the place**
3. **Possible story connections**
4. **Questions to confirm**

The goal is to find possibilities, not to invent romance.
