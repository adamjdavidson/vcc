# The Passion Economy Frame for Cheesemakers

**Purpose:** The economic frame: artisan cheese resists commoditization through specificity, judgment, and story.

This workshop is grounded in Adam Davidson’s *The Passion Economy*.

The core economic idea is that many small businesses cannot win by being cheaper, bigger, faster, or more standardized than industrial competitors. They win by creating value that is specific, hard to copy, and deeply meaningful to the right customers.

## Cheese and the commodity trap

Commodity cheese wins through scale, consistency, convenience, low cost, and repeatability.

Artisan cheese wins differently.

Artisan cheese may be shaped by:

- animal life,
- milk quality,
- seasonality,
- weather,
- land,
- maker judgment,
- cheese tradition,
- risk,
- aging,
- sensory complexity,
- community,
- and a customer’s desire for a richer food experience.

The market, however, often flattens that richness.

A cheese may be reduced to:

- cow / goat / sheep,
- cheddar / blue / bloomy / washed rind,
- age,
- format,
- price,
- and three generic adjectives.

That reduction pushes the cheese toward commodity comparison.

## What story does economically

Story helps people understand the value they cannot see at a glance.

It helps customers and intermediaries understand why this cheese is not interchangeable with a cheaper alternative.

It helps a cheesemonger explain the cheese across the counter.

It helps a buyer justify shelf space.

It helps a consumer feel more pleasure, interest, and confidence when eating and sharing the cheese.

## Do not be a commodity

The LLM should help the cheesemaker resist commoditization.

Do not write language that could describe any Vermont cheese.

Do not write language that could describe any small farm.

Do not hide the maker’s real specificity under generic praise.

Find the story that only this cheese, this maker, this farm, this creamery, or this place could plausibly tell.
