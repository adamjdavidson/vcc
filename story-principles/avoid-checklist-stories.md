# Avoid Checklist Stories

**Purpose:** Why story is not a list of features, and how to find a unifying narrative instead.

A common LLM failure is the checklist story.

It sounds like this:

> This cheese is special because of the milk. It is also special because of the animals. It is also special because of the weather. It is also special because of the geography. It is also special because of the maker’s passion.

This is not a story.

It is a pile of facts.

## Why checklist stories fail

Checklist stories fail because they do not show relationships among facts.

They do not create movement.

They do not help the audience remember what matters.

They often sound like generic marketing copy with more nouns.

## What to do instead

Look for how facts explain one another.

Ask:

- Does the maker’s journey explain why this cheese exists?
- Does the place explain the milk, the method, or the business model?
- Does the cheese tradition solve a problem that still matters here?
- Does the aging process reveal the maker’s judgment?
- Does the customer reaction reveal what the cheese means in use?
- Does a regional challenge explain why the creamery was built this way?
- Does the cheese style give the maker a way to adapt an old solution to a new place?

## Good story behavior

A good story may include many facts, but the facts should feel connected.

Each detail should answer one of these questions:

- Why does this cheese exist?
- Why does it taste or behave this way?
- Why did the maker make this choice?
- Why does this place matter?
- Why should this audience care?
- Why is this cheese worth a premium?

If a detail is true but does not help the story, leave it out or save it for a secondary asset.
