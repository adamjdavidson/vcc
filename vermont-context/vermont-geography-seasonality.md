# Vermont Geography and Seasonality

**Purpose:** Vermont's five distinct dairy landscapes (Champlain Valley, Connecticut River Valley, Piedmont, Green Mountains, Northeast Kingdom) and how to use place as story rather than scenery.

Vermont place stories should be concrete.

Do not write “rolling hills” unless the hills matter.

Do not write “nestled in the Green Mountains” unless the exact location and terrain matter.

## What to research

When given a town, county, region, farm address, creamery address, or milk-source location, research:

- topography,
- elevation,
- rivers and watersheds,
- valleys and mountain ranges,
- soil and pasture context,
- local agriculture,
- weather and seasonality,
- winter constraints,
- summer pasture or haymaking,
- humidity and aging conditions if relevant,
- local transportation or market access,
- nearby towns, co-ops, creameries, farms, or food culture.

## How geography might matter

Geography may shape:

- forage,
- milk seasonality,
- herd management,
- aging humidity,
- winter workload,
- road access,
- local markets,
- tourism,
- farm economics,
- relationships among farms, creameries, and retailers.

But none of these should be assumed. Ask the maker to confirm.

## Good place writing

Weak:

> This cheese comes from the beautiful hills of Vermont.

Better:

> The milk comes from a herd in the White River valley, where spring pasture arrives late and winter feed still shapes the first batches of the season.

The better version is only usable if confirmed by the maker or reliable sources.

## Place as story, not scenery

Place should not merely decorate the story.

Ask how the place acts on the cheese, the maker, the business, or the customer experience.

## Vermont's distinct dairy landscapes

Five regions are worth knowing by name when a cheesemaker provides a specific town or county. These are general orientations, not facts about any specific cheese:

- **Champlain Valley** (west, along Lake Champlain): warmest, flattest, limestone-rich clay soils. Closest Vermont gets to corn country. Home to Shelburne Farms.
- **Connecticut River Valley** (eastern edge): floodplain hay grounds, the river border with New Hampshire.
- **Vermont Piedmont** (central-east): classic hill-farm country, narrow valleys and rolling pasture.
- **Green Mountains** (north-south spine): the higher, steeper interior.
- **Northeast Kingdom** (NEK; northeast corner, named by Senator George Aiken in 1949): highest, coldest, most forested, most stubborn. Home to Jasper Hill (Greensboro), Lazy Lady Farm, and others.

Confirm any regional claim with the cheesemaker before using it. "Champlain Valley" or "Northeast Kingdom" should not become decorative shorthand any more than "Vermont" should.
