# Vermont Place and Season — Story Facts by Region

**Purpose:** What Vermont's distinct dairy landscapes (Champlain Valley, Connecticut River Valley, Vermont Piedmont, Green Mountains, Northeast Kingdom) actually do to the cheese — through soil, climate, what animals thrive there, what they eat, and how the milk shifts season-to-season. **Selective-load** — see `deep-research/README.md`.

> **General orientation, not authority.** Specifics below — soil names, growing-season ranges, breed-region pairings, producer-region attributions — come from synthesized research and are not all independently verified. Each cheesemaker is the authority on her own land. Confirm before claiming any of these specifics in a finished story.

## How to use this file

A cheesemaker is in *one* region. **Load only that region's section.** Optionally load the cross-cutting seasonality section if seasonality is part of the cheese's story. Do not summarize the whole file. Do not mix regions.

The five regions are functional dairy landscapes, not strict political boundaries. Some farms sit at the edges between regions — flag the ambiguity rather than forcing a placement.

## Champlain Valley

**Soil and climate.** The warmest, flattest, most agriculturally favorable part of Vermont. Limy clay soils sit over **Iberville Shale** — a 500-million-year-old rock formation streaked with calcium carbonate that some Vermont cheesemongers credit with the savory mineral edge in long-aged Vermont cheddar (the "Vermont Bite"). Lake Champlain moderates temperatures: later autumn frosts, earlier last-frost dates, and the **longest growing season in Vermont** (typically 150-180 days, depending on the year and exact location). Annual snowfall is lower than the rest of the state; mud season is shorter.

**What thrives.** This is the closest thing Vermont has to corn country. Larger Holstein operations work here when row crops can support them. Smaller heritage breeds (Brown Swiss at Shelburne Farms, Jerseys, Ayrshires) work too and were historically common when butterfat mattered for butter and farmstead cheese.

**Forage.** Cool-season grass mixes (timothy, orchardgrass, brome, fescue) plus white and red clover. The longer growing season means a longer pasture window. Some farms grow alfalfa or corn for silage where flatter ground allows.

**Seasonality of milk.** Spring pasture comes earlier here than anywhere else in Vermont — typically late April or early May at low elevation. The fall pasture window also runs later, sometimes into early November. The carotene-yellow shift in spring milk arrives earlier in the calendar than in higher-elevation regions.

**Producer anchors.** Shelburne Farms (Shelburne) makes farmstead cheddar from its single Brown Swiss herd. Blue Ledge Farm (Salisbury) runs an Alpine and La Mancha goat herd on the blue-rock ledges that give the farm its name.

## Connecticut River Valley

**Soil and climate.** Floodplain hay grounds along the Connecticut River — among the richest agricultural soils in Vermont. The valley itself runs warmer and more humid than the surrounding hills. Once you climb out of the river bottom, you're back in cooler, shorter-season terrain. Growing season along the river is comparable to the Champlain Valley; in the hills above the valley it shortens quickly.

**What thrives.** Sheep have a long history here — Vermont Shepherd in Putney is the modern continuation of that tradition, with David Major having drawn on French Pyrenees technique. Cows do well in the valley bottoms. Mixed operations are common.

**Forage.** Floodplain hay has historically been Vermont's prized winter feed. Pasture composition is similar to the rest of the state (cool-season grasses + clover) but the bottoms support taller, denser growth than the hills.

**Seasonality of milk.** Long pasture window along the river itself; shorter and later as you climb. Vermont Shepherd's *Verano* is strictly spring-summer because the Major family chooses not to make it on hay milk. Parish Hill makes cheese only when the cows at Elm Lea Farm are on hillside pasture — pasture-season production, by design.

**Producer anchors.** Vermont Shepherd (Putney) — sheep on intensively rotational pasture. Parish Hill Creamery (Westminster West) — cow's milk from Elm Lea Farm, autochthonous starters, pasture-only.

## Vermont Piedmont

**Soil and climate.** Classic hill-farm country. Narrow valleys and rolling pasture between the Green Mountains and the Connecticut River. Glacial till soils, often shallow and stony — the **Tunbridge soil series** (Vermont's official state soil since 1985) is most strongly associated with this region. Mid-range elevations, mostly between 500 and 1500 feet. Mud season is real here — six weeks when dirt roads turn to soup. Growing season is shorter than the Champlain Valley but longer than the highest elevations (typically 120-150 days).

**What thrives.** Smaller heritage breeds suited to hill terrain — Jerseys, Brown Swiss, Ayrshires. The classic Vermont hill-dairy farm. Hand-milked herds were 15-25 cows historically; modern small dairies often run similar numbers. Goats do well in the rougher hills, where their browsing habits suit the woody-edge terrain.

**Forage.** Pasture-dominant. Stony soils don't favor row crops, so cool-season grasses and legumes carry the year. Farms diversify into haymaking on what flat ground they have. Hill pastures often include native plants — wild raspberries, asters, dandelion, goldenrod — that goats in particular forage on.

**Seasonality of milk.** Pasture green-up typically mid-May; pasture continues into October. The season-by-season character changes are pronounced because the pasture-to-hay transition is more compressed than in the Champlain Valley.

**Producer anchors.** Spring Brook Farm Cheese (Reading) — raw Jersey-cow milk for *Tarentaise*, a French Alpine-style cheese. Crowley Cheese (Healdville, on the western edge of the Piedmont as it transitions toward the Green Mountains) — continuously operating since 1882. Ayers Brook Goat Dairy (Randolph) — Vermont Creamery's research farm, the nation's first goat-cheese demonstration farm.

## Green Mountains

**Soil and climate.** The spine of the state, north to south, plus the west and central slopes that descend toward the Champlain Valley and the Mad River Valley. Highest elevations in Vermont; metamorphosed Appalachian bedrock with thinner glacial till on top. Heavy snowfall — well over 100 inches a year is common at higher elevations. Mud season can be long. Growing season at higher elevations is short (often 100-120 days); on west and central slopes it's longer.

**What thrives.** Smaller, hardier breeds. Goats in particular do well — they're browsers, comfortable with woody scrub, brambles, and steep terrain. Cattle herds tend to be smaller and lower in the valleys between the ridges. The Mad River Valley supports a range of family-scale dairy operations.

**Forage.** High-elevation pastures support the cool-season grass mix but are slower to green up and earlier to senesce. Forest edges and woody browse are abundant — relevant for goats. Hay availability varies by farm; some Green Mountain farms buy in hay from valley operations to stretch the winter.

**Seasonality of milk.** The latest spring and the earliest fall in the state, after the NEK. Pasture window may run only mid-May to late September at higher elevations. Milk character shifts are pronounced because pasture-to-hay transitions are compressed.

**Producer anchors.** Twig Farm (West Cornwall, on the west slope as it descends into the Champlain Valley) — about 50 Alpine goats foraging on bedrock-ledge pasture; the maker adapts cheese-making parameters daily to whatever the season's milk delivers. von Trapp Farmstead (Waitsfield, Mad River Valley) — organic family-farm creamery making *Mt. Alice*, *Oma*, *Savage*, and *Mad River Blue*.

## Northeast Kingdom (NEK)

**Soil and climate.** The highest, coldest, most forested, most stubborn part of Vermont. Caledonia, Essex, and Orleans counties. Glacial till soils, often thin and stony. The shortest growing season in the state — typically **90 to 110 days** versus 150-plus in the Champlain Valley. Heaviest snowfall. Roads are remote. Senator George Aiken named it the "Northeast Kingdom" in a 1949 speech, and the framing has stuck.

**What thrives.** Hardy breeds. Ayrshires (Jasper Hill's herd) for cold tolerance and pasture efficiency. Goats — Alpine, Oberhasli, Saanen — at smaller operations like Barn First and Lazy Lady. Holsteins where larger operations have the land. The breed mix in the NEK tilts toward foraging hardiness more than peak milk volume.

**Forage.** Cool-season grass mix dominates, but the season is short. Native plants — buckthorn in summer, goldenrod in autumn — feature heavily for goats. **Hay quality matters a lot here** because the winter feeding window is the longest in Vermont.

**Seasonality of milk.** The most extreme seasonality in the state. Pasture green-up may not happen until **late May or early June**; first frost can come in early September. The contrast between spring milk and winter milk is more pronounced than anywhere else in Vermont. Cheesemakers here often build seasonality directly into their recipes — some cheeses are made only on pasture milk; others reflect the winter shift in the paste, the rind, or the timing of aging.

**Producer anchors.** Jasper Hill Farm (Greensboro) — the rocky hillside dairy whose Cellars now age cheese for partner creameries across the region. Lazy Lady Farm (Westfield) — off-grid on wind and solar, subterranean cave cooled by the earth, founded by Laini Fondiller in 1987 (or 1993 by some accounts). Barn First Creamery (Westfield) — Velazquez and Backus running an Alpine and Oberhasli goat herd from a retrofitted hayloft above the milking parlor.

## Cross-cutting Vermont seasonality

These patterns apply statewide, with regional variation in timing and intensity. **Use only when seasonality is genuinely part of the cheese's story** — don't impose a seasonality narrative on a year-round commodity-style cheese.

**The spring shift.** When animals first hit fresh pasture in spring, several things change at once in the milk. **Beta-carotene** from new green growth turns the paste yellower (especially noticeable in cow's milk; less so in goat's milk, which converts carotene to colorless vitamin A). Fat and protein levels are typically lower than later-season milk because animals are gaining condition and producing volume. The flavor brightens — sweeter, grassier, more delicate.

**Summer pasture.** July and August are peak growing season. Pasture composition shifts toward what's flowering and seeding. Hilly pastures with diverse botany give milk that takes on a wider range of aromatic compounds. Cheeses made from summer milk are often more herbaceous, more complex, more variable batch-to-batch.

**Fall concentration.** As pasture growth slows and animals start to put on winter weight, milk gets richer — higher in fat, higher in protein, more concentrated flavor. Some cheesemakers describe fall milk as the best milk of the year for aging cheese, though this varies by style and animal.

**Winter milk.** Animals are on stored hay (and sometimes silage). The milk loses much of the carotene yellow and seasonal complexity, becoming more uniform. Cheesemakers either lean into this — a denser, simpler winter cheese — or pause production until pasture returns. Some cheeses (Vermont Shepherd's *Verano*, Parish Hill's pasture-only cheeses) are strictly seasonal because the producer chooses not to make them on hay milk.

**Mud season.** Six weeks (typically late March through early May) when frozen ground thaws and dirt roads become impassable. Calves are often born in mud season. Milk pickup can be disrupted in remote regions. For a Vermont cheese story, mud season is rarely the focus — but it shapes the calendar around it.

**The aged-wheel paradox.** A wheel of cheese aged 10 months captures a specific season's milk forever. A spring-milk wheel made in May tastes different from a fall-milk wheel made in October, even if the recipe is identical. Some producers explicitly mark vintages or batches by season. Others let it ride and accept the variation as part of the cheese's character.

## How to use this file in a story

- The cheesemaker's worksheet says she's in [town]. Use the table at the top to identify her region, **load only that region's section**, and use the cross-cutting seasonality section if seasonality is part of the story.
- Don't make place-and-season claims about a specific cheese without the cheesemaker's confirmation. *Generally* the Champlain Valley has a longer growing season; *this* cheesemaker's specific pasture, animals, and practices may or may not match the regional average.
- The strongest place-and-season stories explain how the **cheese itself** changed because of where and when it was made — not just that the cheese is from a beautiful place. Useful question stems: *Does spring milk make this cheese different? Does the herd browse differently in late summer? Does the aging room behave differently in the dry winter air? When did this batch's milk come from?*
- A cheesemaker near a regional boundary (Mad River Valley, the Piedmont/Green-Mountain transition, the Champlain Valley/west-slope edge) should be flagged as such. Don't force a placement.
