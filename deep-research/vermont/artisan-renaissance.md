## Frame

The modern Vermont artisan-cheese era is best read as a strategic answer to the commodity-milk crisis that broke open in the 1980s. Federal parity pricing — the safety net since 1937 — was dismantled by the **1980 Farm Bill** and the **1981 Agricultural Food Act**. Fluid milk was thrust into the volatile free market. By 1990, Vermont had **2,370 dairy farms** (down from 11,206 in 1947). By 2008, **1,097**. The choice for surviving small farms became stark: pivot to value-added agriculture or get out. The artisan renaissance is the pivot.

Today **60–65%** of Vermont's raw milk is used by in-state value-added dairy manufacturing. **158 dairy processors** operated in 2023, up from 95 a decade earlier. Vermont has **45+ cheesemakers** producing **150+ varieties**, with the Vermont Cheese Trail marketing 40+ as visitable destinations.

## The pioneers

- **Shelburne Farms cheddar program (1980).** Marshall Webb and Bill Clapp press the first wheels — raw-milk, four ingredients, aged six months to four years, from the farm's own Brown Swiss herd. Shelburne now produces about **170,000 pounds** of cheddar annually.
- **Vermont Creamery (1984).** Allison Hooper and Bob Reese launch with $2,400. The trigger: a French chef at a "Restaurateur of the Year" dinner in Stowe asks for high-quality goat cheese. Reese, then at the Vermont Department of Agriculture, finds Hooper. She makes a fresh batch in a converted milk house on a Brookfield farm. They scale by organizing a network of family farms rather than relying on one herd.
- **Vermont Shepherd (1993).** David and Yesenia Major's Putney farm faces the wool-subsidy collapse. David travels to the French Pyrenees, returns with sheep's-milk technique, launches *Verano*. The family digs its own subterranean aging cave into a Vermont hillside. They run intensive rotational grazing, moving sheep to fresh pasture every twelve hours; the flock browses up to 135 native plant species. *Verano* is strictly seasonal (spring–summer pasture only). **2000**: Vermont Shepherd wins Best of Show at the American Cheese Society — first sheep's-milk cheese to do so. Kicks off a regional sheep-dairying vogue.
- **Lazy Lady Farm / Laini Fondiller (1993).** Engages in a public legal battle with Vermont's Department of Agriculture over regulations requiring separate facilities for pasteurization and cheesemaking — a financial impossibility for small farmstead producers. Loses the case but draws the public attention that eventually leads to regulatory reform.
- **Peter Dixon — "the King of Cheese."** Returns to his family's Guilford farm in 1982 after his father's raw-milk bottling business is shut down by regulation. Studies dairy science at UVM, trains in France. Becomes the recipe-development consultant behind Shelburne Farms cheddar; Vermont Creamery's *Chevrier* and fontina; Taylor Farm's Gouda; and the *Dorset* and *Manchester* wheels at Consider Bardwell. **2013**: Dixon and Rachel Fritz Schaal launch **Parish Hill Creamery** in Westminster West, committing exclusively to natural starters and traditional raw-milk fermentation.
- **Jasper Hill Farm (1998).** Brothers Andy and Mateo Kehler buy a derelict hillside dairy farm in Greensboro (Northeast Kingdom) — an area that had lost **30% of its dairies in a single year**. They build an Ayrshire herd and an on-site microbiology lab.

## The infrastructure plays

- **Vermont Cheese Council (1996).** Founded by Hooper and Reese. 19 original members; 50+ by 2015. Provides shared identity through quality production, safety training, and state-level branding.
- **Vermont Institute for Artisan Cheese at UVM (2004).** Built around **Paul Kindstedt** and **Catherine Donnelly**. Not just a promotional body — VIAC is a serious research hub on Listeria, cheese safety, and artisan microbiology. Donnelly later serves as editor in chief of *The Oxford Companion to Cheese*.
- **Cabot Clothbound Cheddar (2006).** Cabot makes excellent cheddar but lacks dedicated cave space for natural rinds. Jasper Hill ages it. The collaboration wins **Best of Show at the 2006 ACS competition** — the moment that proves big cooperative milk can sit inside artisan affinage.
- **The Cellars at Jasper Hill (2008).** A **22,000-square-foot underground affinage facility** with **seven vaulted tunnels**, holding up to one million pounds of cheese. Designed to age cheese for partner creameries, not just for Jasper Hill. Affinage is roughly 70% of the labor in cheesemaking; centralizing it eradicated the barrier to entry for small neighboring farmsteads. Modern equivalent of Cabot's 1919 shared boiler.

## The terroir science

The romance has a science layer underneath:

- **The "Vermont Bite."** Tom Perry of Shelburne Farms attributes Vermont cheddar's signature sharpness and savory depth to the **Iberville Shale**, a 500-million-year-old rock formation under the Champlain Valley, streaked with calcium carbonate. The shale leaches calcite into Vermont soil; the mineral passes through pasture into milk; aging expresses it as sulfuric aroma and umami-sharp flavor. Verify with the cheesemaker before claiming this for any specific cheese.
- **Biodiverse rotational forage.** Twelve-hour pasture rotations across pastures with 130+ native plant species (Vermont Shepherd's practice) yield dense, herbaceous, season-shifting milk.
- **Autochthonous starters.** Parish Hill cultivates indigenous bacterial cultures from the raw, clabbered milk of four specific cows in the Elm Lea Farm herd — **Sonia, Helga, Clothilde, and Abigail**. Rachel Fritz Schaal: *"Think about the difference between sourdough and conventional bread. Then imagine there are only five sourdough bread bakers in the United States."*
- **On-site microbiology labs.** Jasper Hill runs a working lab studying how soil, pasture, and cave environment shape its raw-milk ecology. UVM Extension has used Profile Attribute Analysis (sensory-against-instrument matching) to map the specific flavors consumers respond to in Jasper Hill and Spring Brook cheeses.

## The competitive record

Vermont cheese is a globally competitive ecosystem, not a single celebrity brand:

- **Bayley Hazen Blue (Jasper Hill).** Won **"World's Best Unpasteurized Cheese"** at the World Cheese Awards in **2014**. In **2024**, named **Best American Cheese** and placed in the world Top 14 (out of 4,786 entries) by the Guild of Fine Food.
- **Vermont Creamery's aged goat cheeses** — *Cremont*, *Bonne Bouche*, *Coupole*, *Bijou* — repeatedly win Super Gold and Gold at the World Cheese Awards. *Cremont* and *Bijou* won **Good Food Awards in 2022**.
- **2023**: Vermont cheesemakers collectively win **142 domestic and international awards** — 44 at ACS, 24 at International Cheese & Dairy Awards, 23 at World Cheese Awards, 51 at The Big E.
- **2025 ACS competition (Sacramento)**: Vermont producers earn **49 ribbons**. Jasper Hill places second in Best in Show with *Withersbrook Blue*; takes first across categories with *Bayley Hazen Blue*, *Harbison*, and *Alpha Tolman*. Grafton wins first for *Clothbound Cheddar*. Cabot dominates butter and Jack categories.
- **2025 World Cheese Awards (Bern)**: Vermont takes 33 awards including 2 Super Golds.
- **Grafton Village's** *Shepsog* and *Bismark* — Super Gold at World Cheese Awards (2012, 2014).
- **Parish Hill's** *Humble* and *Idyll* — Super Gold WCA 2023; *Reverie* — Gold 2024.

The state-level message: Vermont is more persuasive as an ecosystem than as any single brand.

## How Vermont cheese travels

The state's identity is built around the cheese plus the experience around it:

- **Vermont Cheese Trail** — 40+ visitable producers with farm stores, viewing windows, public access.
- **Diverse business models** under one state brand: farmstead (Shelburne), creamery sourcing from a network (Vermont Creamery), affineur hub (Jasper Hill), cooperative legacy (Cabot, Grafton), educational nonprofit (Spring Brook / Farms for City Kids), wild-fermentation purist (Parish Hill).
- **Tourism + product**: many Vermont cheeses are sold not just as food but as place, visit, and experience. Vermont's $113M in dairy exports (2022) flows through this brand even though most exports aren't artisan-cheese-specific.
