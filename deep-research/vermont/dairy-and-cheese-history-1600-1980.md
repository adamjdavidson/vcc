## Frame

The land made dairy inevitable: glacial till and cool-season grass favored cows over grain. Vermonters then made dairy into an identity — first farmhouse cheese and butter, then commodity butter and fluid milk for Boston. The commodity economy nearly broke the small farm. A counterculture wave in the 1970s seeded the artisan revival that would arrive after 1980.

## The five Vermont dairy landscapes

- **Champlain Valley** (west) — warmest, flattest, limy clay soils. Vermont's nearest thing to corn country. Home of Shelburne Farms.
- **Connecticut River Valley** (east) — floodplain hay grounds, the river border with New Hampshire.
- **Vermont Piedmont** (central-east) — classic hill-farm country, narrow valleys, rolling pasture.
- **Green Mountains** — the spine, north to south.
- **Northeast Kingdom** ("NEK") — highest, coldest, most forested, most stubborn. Named by Senator George Aiken in a 1949 speech. Home today of Jasper Hill (Greensboro) and others.

The climate verdict: marginal for the annual field crops dairy cattle are typically fed, but ideal for cool-season grasses and legumes. Tunbridge soil — the Vermont state soil since 1985 — is twenty to forty inches of glacial till over schist and gneiss. Dairy was less a choice than a verdict the land delivered.

## The sheep that built the pastures (1810s–1850)

- **1810–1811**: William Jarvis, U.S. Consul to Lisbon, ships ~3,630 prized Merinos to his Weathersfield Bow estate. Brings Spanish shepherds and dogs. (His gravestone is carved with a Merino in bas-relief.)
- **1840 peak**: ~1,681,819 sheep against ~280,000 Vermonters — six sheep per Vermonter. Merino rams sold for $1,500–$5,000 apiece when a common sheep brought two dollars.
- **Sheep ate the forest.** Pre-European cover was >95% trees; by 1880, just over a third remained.
- **The walls.** Roughly 240,000 miles of New England stone walls — about six linear miles per square mile of land — were built to enclose sheep. The walls now running through Vermont's reforested woods were built for sheep but outlined the pastures cows would later graze.
- **1846–1850 collapse**: Erie Canal opens cheap Ohio pasture; tariffs fall; two-thirds of Vermont's sheep are killed in four years. The cleared, walled hillsides became cow pasture.

## The cows (1623 onward)

- **1623**: first European cattle in New England (Plymouth, Devonshire). Milking Devons (the "Ruby Reds") and undocumented "native" or "scrub" cattle including Linebacks and the now-vanishing Cream Pots.
- **Randall Linebacks** were nearly extinct: the last fifteen were found in 1985 on Everett Randall's farm in Sunderland.
- **1871**: Frederick Billings imports Jerseys to Woodstock, founding what becomes Billings Farm. Jersey herds from West Randolph win first prize for butter at the **1890 Paris Exposition** and again at the **1893 World's Columbian Exposition**.
- **1903**: the Holstein-Friesian Association of America moves to Brattleboro. Still there, at One Holstein Place.
- **The rhythm**: twice a day, 365 days a year. As one Vermont mother told her son the day they sold the herd, "you spend two to three hours twice a day with them — that's more than a lot of people spend with their kids."

## Cheese as women's work, then factory work

- **1623–1820s**: English women bring cheese knowledge from the cheddar regions of southwest England. The dairy room — a stone-floored, north-facing, cool room often built over a spring — is the woman's domain.
- **1824**: Crowley family begins cooperative cheesemaking in Healdville. Decades before any "factory" existed.
- **1851**: Jesse Williams opens the first true cheese factory in Rome, NY — combining the milk of his and his son's farms to produce 100,000 pounds in a season. The model sweeps north. By 1900 Vermont has **186 creameries and 66 cheese factories**.
- **1869** peak farmhouse year: Vermont produces **4,830,700 pounds** of cheese, almost all by women in farm kitchens, by recipes that traveled in heads and notebooks.
- **1880** peak farm count: over **35,000 farms**.
- **1882**: A. Winfield Crowley builds the Healdville factory beside the family farm. Continuously operating since (added to the National Register in 1979). Crowley cheese is technically a washed-curd cheddar — the technique the USDA later named "Colby" after a Wisconsin town. Cynthia Dawley, longtime general manager: "We're still making the same cheese in the same way."

## Butter trains and milk trains (1850s–1972)

- **1854**: first weekly iced butter train runs from St. Albans to Boston.
- **1872**: R.R. Sherman in St. Albans on a Tuesday — "the hotels are full and the farmers, I mean their pockets, are full. **Butter is king.**" (The Confederate raiders who hit St. Albans in October 1864 picked Wednesday — the day after the butter trains had filled the bank vaults.)
- **1880s**: refrigerated rail cars open the Boston fluid-milk market. Boston's annual fluid-milk consumption doubles between 1911 and 1921.
- **By 1915**: nearly 300 butter factories operating in Vermont; peak ~**20.4 million pounds**. The Franklin County Creamery in St. Albans was rated the largest butter factory in the world in the 1890s — 10 tons of butter a day from 20,000 cows.
- **1972**: last bulk milk train to Boston runs in August. Trucks take over.

## Cooperatives as Vermont's structural reflex

- **1868** (or **1872** — sources differ): Vermont Dairymen's Association formed; first of its kind in the United States.
- **1892**: Grafton Cooperative Cheese Company founded by farmers pooling surplus raw milk.
- **April 1, 1919**: 94 Cabot-area farmers buy back their failing creamery for **$3,700** — **$5 per cow** plus **a cord of wood** to fuel the boiler. Combined herds: 863 cows. Harry Walbridge famously hauled his daily milk in a wheelbarrow (one cow). The **McDonald Farm in Danville**, settled by Scottish immigrants in 1839, is the sole survivor among the 94 founding families — now in its seventh generation through James Beattie.
- The Cabot founding ritual — five dollars a cow plus a cord of wood — has become almost mythic among Vermont dairy families. It captures something specific: that **banding together against distant markets is older than the state's dairy identity**.

## The Webbs and Shelburne Farms

- **1886**: Eliza "Lila" Vanderbilt Webb and Dr. William Seward Webb begin buying 33 farms, assembling a 3,800-acre estate seven miles south of Burlington. They engage **Frederick Law Olmsted** for the landscape and **Robert Henderson Robertson** for the buildings.
- The result: the 110-room **Shelburne House**; the U-shaped **Farm Barn** under two acres of roof; the 418-foot **Breeding Barn** — largest open-span wooden agricultural structure in America at the time.
- Dr. Webb's bet: that **horses** would be the future of Vermont agriculture. The internal combustion engine called the bet wrong.
- **1950s**: Derick Webb (grandson) establishes a Brown Swiss herd "for hardiness, foraging ability, milk quality, longevity and temperament." That herd's milk still goes into Shelburne Farms cheddar today.
- **1972**: Derick's six children found Shelburne Farms Resources as an environmental-education nonprofit.
- **1980**: **Marshall Webb and Bill Clapp** press the first wheels of the Shelburne Farms cheddar program — raw-milk, four ingredients only, aged six months to four years.

## How "Vermont" became a brand

- The line "Vermont has more cows than people" is **more cherished than literally true**. It was **sheep**, not cows, that outnumbered Vermonters six to one in 1840. Dairy cows peaked around **290,000** against a human population of **359,000 in 1930** — Vermonters always outnumbered their cows in the state total. (The myth is honest in spirit if loose in arithmetic.)
- **1946**: *Vermont Life* magazine is founded by the State of Vermont as a marketing tool, modeled on *Life*. Editor Tom Slayton, later: aimed at convincing readers "we were 'a special world.'"
- **1877** Vermont Board of Agriculture official report: *"Poor butter is an abomination; to eat it is to degrade the physical, intellectual and moral nature of man."*
- **The honest complication**: the same intellectual project that produced "the hardy Vermonter" also produced the **Eugenics Survey of Vermont** under UVM's Henry F. Perkins, which led to Vermont's 1931 sterilization law and targeted French Canadians, Abenaki, and rural poor families. The Yankee-purity branding has this shadow.

## The squeeze (1947–1980)

- **1947 peak**: 11,206 dairy farms.
- **1952**: bulk tanks begin to be pushed onto small farms by milk handlers. Within a decade required.
- **1966 alone**: over **900 Vermont dairy farms** shut down.
- Don George (former head of Vermont's Dairy Division) on the daily milk-can drop at the creamery: *"This was the way the farmer communicated with all of his neighboring farmers. There was a lot of chitchat that went on about dairy farming and other things."* The bulk tank ended that.
- Earl Butz era: **"get big or get out."** Jack Lazor of Butterworks Farm: in **1982–83**, "three or four farm auctions a week."
- **By 1980** the entire Vermont artisan-cheese landscape consisted of: **Crowley, Plymouth, Cabot, Grafton, and the brand-new Shelburne Farms cheddar program**. That was it. But that was the beginning.

## The seeds underneath

- **1970–80**: an estimated **40,000 "flatlanders"** move to Vermont. State population (stagnant for a century) jumps 15% in the 1970s. Of seven hundred surveyed, **75% had college degrees** in a state where most natives had not been to college.
- They came mainly **for food**. As VHS curator Jackie Calder put it: *"If one single unifying theme could connect the entirety of the counterculture experience in 1970s Vermont, it would be food."*
- **1971**: **NOFA** — Northeast Organic Farming Association — co-founded out of this milieu.
- The communes (Total Loss Farm in Guilford, Mullein Hill in Glover, Earth People's Park in Norton) mostly faded by mid-decade. They left an infrastructure: food co-ops (Plainfield, Hunger Mountain, Buffalo Mountain, Onion River), farmers' markets, organic-farm networks.
- Kate Daloz, *We Are As Gods*: *"Just because we didn't end up with what we thought we were going to end up with doesn't mean we ended up with nothing."*
