## Why hard goat cheese is the hard problem

- **Goat was the "poor man's cow."** Goats are selective foragers, not grazers — they thrive on rough scrub, woody brush, bramble, and rocky terrain that won't support a heavy bovine herd. Small-scale goat dairying persisted on the rugged margins of European mountains long after cows took over the valley pastures.
- **The Black Death changed everything.** Pre-plague, sheep and goats dominated Alpine grazing because they were cheaper and more agile. The 14th-century labor shortage flipped the equation: cows produce far more milk per animal per labor-hour. The cooked-pressed wheels that became globally famous — Emmental, Gruyère, Appenzeller, Beaufort, Comté — were developed and scaled around **cow's milk**, not goat. The *Armailli* (specialized professional cheesemakers/cowherds who managed summer high-pastures) are a cow story.
- **Goat milk fights the wheel.** The biochemistry:
  - **Low alpha-s1 casein** — goat curds are fragile, soft, prone to retaining moisture. Cow curds are firm and easily pressed.
  - **High medium-chain triglycerides** — rapid lipid oxidation creates "soapy," "goaty," "lemon pepper" off-flavors if milk isn't processed fast.
  - **Low carotene** — goat cheese paste is snowy white; cow paste is sunny yellow.
- **Most historical goat cheese is lactic-set, not pressed.** Loire Valley *chèvre* — gravity-drained into molds, no mechanical pressing, high-moisture, short-lived — is the dominant ancient goat-cheese form for biochemical reasons, not preference.
- **The exceptions prove the rule.** Hard goat cheeses do exist in alpine traditions: *Chevrotin* (Savoie), *Garrotxa* (Catalonia), *Majorero* (Canary Islands). They demand precise handling, rapid processing, and careful pressing. They show that with skill, goat milk *can* be transformed into a dense, age-worthy mountain wheel — but it's craft against biochemistry, not with it.

## Vermont alpine-goat producers

Use cautiously — confirm with the cheesemaker before claiming any of this for *their* specific cheese.

- **Twig Farm** (West Cornwall) — Michael Lee and Emily Sunderman, est. 2005. ~50 Alpine goats (plus a few Saanens and Nubians) on 20 acres dominated by shallow bedrock — the "ledges." Mechanical mowing impossible; botanical diversity high. Lee adapts make-parameters daily to whatever the season's milk delivers. Cheeses:
  - *Old Goat* — 5-10 lbs, 10-12 months in the farm cellar; firm flaky ivory paste, native rind molds, sweet/nutty/caramel notes that subvert the "goat = sharp" expectation.
  - *Goat Tomme* — 80-day version of the same logic.
  - *Mixed Drum* — goat plus high-butterfat Ayrshire cow milk from neighboring Crawford Family Farm; Pyrenees/Alps mixed-milk lineage.
- **Barn First Creamery** (Westfield, NEK; population ~500) — Rebecca Velazquez and Merlin Backus. Live in a 3,800 sq ft retrofitted hayloft directly above the milking parlor. Velazquez self-taught from historical cheese manuals; reverse-engineered cow's-milk recipes for goat milk. Cheeses named after women in the family who didn't pass on their surnames.
  - *Malloy* — won first place at the 2019 ACS competition.
  - *Urdang* — square-format washed rind; dense, fudgy paste with chalky tangy core.
- **Blue Ledge Farm** (Salisbury) — Hannah Sessions and Greg Bernhardt; met studying art in Florence. 130 Alpine and La Mancha goats; year-round breeding to keep milk supply continuous (overcoming caprine seasonality).
  - *Lake's Edge* and *Camembrie* are the soft-cheese signatures; *Riley's 2x4* (mixed goat-and-cow) won first in Farmstead Original Mixed Milk at 2024 ACS.
- **Sage Farm Goat Dairy** (Stowe) — Molly and Katie Pindell; seasonal small-batch from a registered Alpine herd.
- **Bridport Creamery** (Bridport) — Nicki Foster, Master Cheesemaker candidate. *Swisserella* — original recipe combining Swiss-alpine nutty aromatics with mozzarella's soft elastic texture.
- **Boston Post Dairy** (Enosburg Falls) — Gervais family. *Très Bonne* — aged goat-milk Gouda-style; nutty, smooth, creamier mouthfeel than goat milk usually offers.
- **Vermont Creamery — *Hooper*** (Websterville) — launched for the creamery's 40th anniversary, named for co-founder Allison Hooper. **Donut shape** to maximize rind-to-paste ratio for even maturation. Mixed milk (goat + cow). The creamery's first washed rind. French gray sea salt brine. Wrinkled *Geotrichum candidum* surface.

## The make-science angle

When the story benefits from explaining *how* alpine-goat is engineered:

- **Thermophilic culture work.** *Lactobacillus helveticus* (named for the Helvetii tribe of Switzerland — the same root as *Caseus Helveticus*, Pliny the Elder's first-century reference to Swiss cheese) does the long proteolytic work that produces sweet, nutty, umami notes during aging. Strains like *Choozit Flav54* break down milk proteins into sweet peptides while suppressing bitter ones. Combined with *S. thermophilus* (which acidifies fast at high cook temperatures), the synergy mutes goat milk's sharper edges and pushes flavor toward the long-aged Gruyère/Comté register.
- **Copper vats.** Twenty times the thermal conductivity of stainless steel; leached copper ions actively shape proteolysis, microbial ecology, and final flavor. AOP regulations for Gruyère, Emmentaler, Sbrinz, Comté, and Parmigiano Reggiano *require* unlined copper. A 2009 Università Cattolica del Sacro Cuore study made eight Parmigiano-recipe batches — four copper, four stainless — and found pristine wheels in copper at 17 months and severe defects in stainless. Copper isn't romance; it's biochemistry.

## Hyper-local affinage in Vermont

Vermont's most distinctive innovation in alpine-goat (and adjacent styles) is washing the rind with **regional liquids** — substituting for the saltwater brine that traditionally cultivates *Brevibacterium linens*:

- **Parish Hill Creamery** — washes *Humble* with hard cider three times a week (gloved hand, dipped in the bowl, rubbed directly on the rind). Native apple-skin microflora cultivate funky, mushroom-like savoriness.
- **Jasper Hill Farm** — uses **Eden Ice Cider** (concentrated by naturally freezing Vermont apple juice in winter cold) to wash *Withersbrook Blue*. Sharp alcohol and floral apple sweetness permeate over months into a fudgy, hazelnut-honey paste. Won major recognition in 2025.
- **Barn First Creamery** — washes triangular caprine cheeses in *Sip of Sunshine* (a Vermont craft IPA). Sunset-colored rind; buttery paste with savory, yeasty, hoppy depth.
- **Crown Finish Caves** — applies local ciders to Nettle Meadow Farm's *Pins & Nettles*; tarragon and sea salt make it land.

The cider/beer wash does two things: regulates moisture and microbial health on the rind biologically; chemically tethers the product to Vermont through novel ester compounds the alcohol generates with surface fats and proteins. Terroir-driven aroma you can't get from milk fermentation alone.

- Reverse-engineered traditional cow's-milk technique for goat milk.
- Reclaimed Jura copper vats.
- Washed in [named local cider / beer].
- Aged 10-12 months (Old Goat) — long for goat.
