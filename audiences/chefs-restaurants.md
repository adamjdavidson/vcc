# Chefs and Restaurants

**Purpose:** Chefs are evaluating an ingredient. Their question is *what does this cheese let me do?* — pairing, behavior, format, menu fit.

## Who they are

A chef's question is **"what does this cheese let me do?"** They evaluate a cheese the way they evaluate any ingredient — by what it contributes on the plate or the board, not by its origin story. The story matters insofar as it can also live on a menu or be told by servers when guests ask.

Chefs are sophisticated about flavor and texture, but they have a kitchen to run. Be useful before you are romantic.

## What they want from a story

- **What the cheese does.** Behavior with heat, behavior over time, melt characteristics, salt level, acidity, how it eats with other foods. They are mentally placing it on a plate.
- **Pairing and menu use cases.** Cheese plate? Stuffed into a tart? Crumbled hot over roasted vegetables? Finishing a risotto? The producer who suggests two or three concrete uses is doing chef's prep work.
- **Format and reliability.** Chefs need to know the cheese will arrive in a usable size, on a predictable schedule, in consistent condition. Variation is interesting; unpredictability is a problem.
- **A line for the menu and the server.** Many menus carry the cheese's name plus a short descriptor. Many servers field "what is this cheese?" tableside. A producer story should give the chef both.
- **Provenance that justifies the menu price.** When the cheese sits at a $24 cheese plate or in a $42 entrée, the chef needs a clean line about why this cheese, not a generic local one.

## How to write for them

- **Lead with use, not origin.** "This cheese melts to a glossy, brothy texture without separating, which makes it ideal as a finish for hot pasta or roasted alliums" is more useful than "this cheese has been made on our farm since 2008."
- **Include format and seasonality directly.** Chefs make menu decisions on a calendar. Tell them when this cheese is at its best and when it changes.
- **Offer two specific use cases.** One safe, one ambitious. Lets the chef calibrate.
- **Provide a menu-line and a server-line.** A 10-12 word menu descriptor and a 20-second server explanation.

## What to avoid

- Pure romance with no practical detail.
- Sensory descriptors that don't map to behavior in the kitchen ("ineffably complex," "transportive").
- Surprise variation. If the cheese shifts dramatically with the season, say so on the front end so the chef can plan around it.

## Example pattern

> *(Menu line)*: Aged farmstead tomme, washed rind, [town] Vermont.
>
> *(Server line)*: It's a gentle washed-rind cheese — the maker rubs it with brine while it ages, which builds the savory flavor without making it strong. Pairs really well with the dish's roasted onions.
>
> *(For the chef)*: Behaves predictably warm — melts smooth, doesn't break. Strongest from June through October when the herd is on pasture.
