# Cheesemongers

**Purpose:** Cheesemongers are sophisticated translators who want depth, technical detail, and farm visits. Don't dumb anything down.

## Who they are

A cheesemonger is a **sophisticated translator**. They have tasted hundreds of cheeses and can place a Vermont alpine next to a French Comté in their head without prompting. They spend their working day converting producer specifics into customer language at the counter — usually in 30 seconds, often without the producer present.

Treat them as peers in cheese knowledge. They are not the audience to dumb anything down for.

## What they want from a story

- **Depth.** Their appetite for technical, environmental, agricultural, and methodological detail is enormous. Specifics about milk source, breed, cultures, rind treatment, aging environment, washing schedule, seasonality, pasture composition — all welcomed, none too much.
- **Memorable hooks they can repeat.** A great monger story gives them a single vivid line they can deploy at the counter (*"this cheese is washed in Eden Ice Cider made twenty miles from the creamery"*) plus the deeper context for the customer who leans in and wants more.
- **A reason to come visit the farm.** Mongers actively look for producers worth visiting. A farm visit is how they deepen their relationship to the cheese, gather material for their own customer storytelling, and build the kind of connection that influences ordering. If a visit is welcome, say so. If not, give them the next best thing — a video walkthrough, a phone call, a detailed product sheet.
- **Honesty about variation.** Mongers handle cheese batch-to-batch and notice changes. Tell them what shifts seasonally — the milk, the paste, the rind — and they'll know how to set customer expectations.
- **The quirks.** What can go wrong with this cheese, what makes it tricky to sell, what it teaches them about a category. They are professional learners.

## How to write for them

- Use the technical vocabulary directly: *thermophilic*, *clothbound*, *autochthonous*, *affinage*, *washed-curd*, *raw-milk*. They know these words.
- Lead with **specifics** — single-herd raw milk; six months on the board; washed three times a week with X; pasture composition shifts in May. Skip the romance unless it's literally true.
- Include the **production context** — who makes it, how long, on what equipment, in what kind of room. They use this to build their own stories.
- Offer the visit. If you can't host, say what you can offer instead.
- Keep the elevator version (15-30 seconds) tight enough to repeat across the counter. Then keep an "ask me more" trail for the curious.

## What to avoid

- Generic luxury-food language. They will see through it instantly and respect the cheese less for it.
- Withholding technical detail to avoid "confusing them." You will not.
- Marketing-speak about "passion" or "tradition" without specifics behind it.

## Example pattern

> This is our washed-rind tomme made from a single herd of Brown Swiss cows two miles from the creamery. We wash it three times a week with a brine seeded from our previous batch, which is part of why it stays gentler than the aroma suggests. The rind builds in May when the cows go on pasture; it gets brothier and more complex through summer. If you ever want to come up and see the wash room, we host monger visits twice a year — let us know.
