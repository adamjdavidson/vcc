# Wholesalers and Distributors

**Purpose:** Wholesalers and distributors are sell-through thinkers one step further removed. Their question is *will retailers buy this from me?* Stories must travel.

## Who they are

A wholesaler or distributor is a step further removed from the eater than a retailer is. Their question is **"will retailers buy this from me, often enough, to make it worth carrying in my book?"** They have to be able to *re-tell* the cheese's story to their own buyer customers — many of whom will never meet the producer in person.

Their judgment of a cheese depends on whether they can move it. They tend to be experienced cheese people, often with strong personal taste, but they are running a business of breadth and turn. The cheese either earns its slot in the catalog or it doesn't.

## What they want from a story

- **A capsule that survives distance.** A story that works when told by someone who has never visited the farm, to a retailer who has never met the maker. Portable. Not dependent on the producer's charm.
- **A clear answer to "who buys this and why."** What kind of retailer does this cheese fit? Specialty grocery? High-end cheese counter? Co-op? Restaurant supply? They are mentally placing it in their book before they decide whether to add it.
- **Differentiation language.** What separates this cheese from the others in its category that they already carry? They need a single line that will earn it space in a catalog or a sales call.
- **Trade specifics they need to share.** Format, case pack, lead time, MOQ, pricing tiers, availability windows, distribution territory, samples, marketing support. The story matters because it generates the distributor's selling conversation; the practicals make the deal possible.
- **Reliability signals.** A wholesaler is investing relationship capital with their retailer customers when they recommend a new cheese. The producer story has to suggest "this one will arrive consistently and won't embarrass me at the next account review."

## How to write for them

- **Build the story as a portable capsule** — three or four sentences a salesperson can tell from memory across a coffee shop table.
- **Pre-package the differentiation.** Don't make the wholesaler invent the "what makes this different" line — give it to them.
- **Name the retailer types this cheese fits.** Help them place it in their book.
- **Be aggressive about the practical mechanics.** Wholesalers respect specifics: format, weight, case pack, lead time, MOQ, terms, allergen info, organic certification status, exclusivity, marketing co-op support.
- **Make the producer story sound capable.** Not glossy — capable. A short note about production volume, food safety standards, and shipping reliability does more than a long passion paragraph.

## What to avoid

- Stories that require the producer to be in the room. If a wholesaler can't tell it without the maker present, it won't travel.
- Delicate sensory nuance that gets lost across three layers of intermediaries. Save the subtle stuff for the consumer copy.
- Long personal biography. Useful in trade-press storytelling, not on a sales sheet.

## Example pattern

> A washed-rind tomme made from a single Brown Swiss herd in [town], Vermont. It's the gentle counterpart to the funkier washed rinds in your book — pairs naturally next to a Vacherin Mont d'Or or Reblochon-style on the case map. Format: 1.5-lb wheels, 12 per case. Ten-day lead time, MOQ one case. We're producing year-round, with a milk-character shift in late spring as the herd hits pasture. Available for retail demo support and a 30-minute video producer briefing for any retailer who places an opening order.
