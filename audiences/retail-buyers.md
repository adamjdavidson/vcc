# Retail Buyers

**Purpose:** Retail buyers are sell-through thinkers. Their question is *will my customers buy this often enough to justify the shelf space?*

## Who they are

A retail buyer's job is to decide what goes in the case. Their core question is **"will my customers buy this, often enough, at this price, to justify the shelf space and the staff training?"** Everything else — the romance of the story, the elegance of the rind, the heritage of the maker — matters only to the extent that it answers that question.

Retail buyers are practical, but they are not mercenary. The good ones are deeply curious about cheese and care about the producers they stock. They just have a P&L and a finite cheese case, and the cheese has to earn its keep.

## What they want from a story

- **A reason their customers will be attracted to this cheese.** Not a reason *they* like it — a reason their *customers* will pick it up. Who is it for? What occasion? What does the customer learn when they ask the staff about it?
- **Repeatable language for their staff.** Their cheesemonger or counter person needs a clean line — about 15-30 seconds — to give a curious customer. The story has to be portable, not poetic.
- **Category fit.** Where does this cheese sit in the case? What does it sit next to? What does it replace or complement? Is it the showstopper, the workhorse, or the quiet specialty?
- **Practical mechanics.** Format, shelf life, lead time, MOQ, sample availability, price tier, whether it cuts cleanly, how it ages on the counter.
- **Confidence the producer is reliable.** Will this cheese arrive when promised, in the same condition, batch after batch? Buyers have been burned. A consistent, professional producer story matters.

## How to write for them

- **Lead with the customer-attraction angle.** "Customers who are curious about washed-rind cheese but worried about funk love this one — it's gentle, but the rind has real character." Skip the maker's biography unless it's directly relevant.
- **Give them a 15-30 second line their staff can use.** Underline it or set it apart so they can find and use it.
- **Compare to cheeses they already know.** Specialty buyers think in cases. Tell them where this cheese fits relative to the cheeses they already stock.
- **Be honest about the practical.** Format, weight, lead time, terms, seasonality. Don't bury this in the romance.
- **Mention the staff-education hook.** A good buyer wants their staff energized about new cheese. A producer who offers a tasting visit, a video, or a phone briefing is doing the buyer a favor.

## What to avoid

- Long emotional founder biographies that don't connect to sell-through.
- Unsupported premium claims that the buyer will have to translate into actual customer reasons. Do that translation for them.
- "Pure passion" language. Buyers translate that into "this producer doesn't yet know how to sell."

## Example pattern

> This is a washed-rind tomme that fits in your case next to your gentler aged cheeses — the customers who like Vacherin Mont d'Or but find Époisses too aggressive will respond to this one. **Staff line: "It smells stronger than it tastes. Let it warm up. The rind is part of the experience."** Format: 1.5 lb wheels, ten-day lead time, MOQ four wheels. We can send a sample and do a 15-minute call with your counter staff to walk through how to talk about it.
