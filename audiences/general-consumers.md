# General Consumers (Artisanal Cheese Buyers)

**Purpose:** Artisanal-cheese consumers — opted in to premium pricing, but need to understand *why* this cheese is worth more. Variable sophistication, open and curious, need storytelling.

## Who they are

Artisanal cheese consumers have **already opted in to premium pricing in principle** — they're at a farmers market, specialty shop, or co-op, not the supermarket aisle. But they need to **reconfirm the value** with each purchase. They are open to paying more for a cheese, and they want to understand *why* it costs more.

Their sophistication varies enormously. Some can name twelve washed-rind cheeses; some can't tell sheep from goat by sight. **All of them are curious.** They are choosing engagement over convenience. Treat them as smart people who haven't yet been told the thing that would make this cheese mean something to them.

## What they want from a story

- **A clear answer to "why is this worth more than the cheese on the next shelf?"** Not as a defensive justification — as a doorway into the cheese.
- **Something to take home and tell someone else.** The story is partly about the cheese tasting better when you know more about it. Eating becomes more engaging when you know that the milk came from one herd, or that the rind is washed daily, or that the cheese only exists in the months the cows are on pasture.
- **Permission to enjoy.** Many consumers feel slightly intimidated by artisanal cheese — worried about choosing wrong, looking unsophisticated, "wasting" a premium product. A good story disarms that.
- **A sensory invitation.** What to notice when they taste it. What to do at home — temperature, pairings, what to expect from the rind. Concrete, specific, repeatable.

## How to write for them

- **Lead with one specific, vivid thing** about the cheese, not with a category claim. Not *"our authentic Vermont cheddar"* but *"this is the cheddar we make slowly enough that the paste starts to break into a clean, savory crumble."*
- **Explain technical vocabulary in passing.** Don't avoid it — define it briefly and move on. *"It's a washed-rind cheese, which means we rub the surface with brine while it ages — that's where the savory smell comes from."* Consumers want to learn the vocabulary; they just don't want a lecture.
- **Use story-shape, not feature-list.** A maker's choice, a place, an animal, a season — show the chain of why this cheese exists and why it tastes the way it does. Five facts that explain each other beat ten facts that don't.
- **Give them the "tell a friend" line.** One thing memorable enough that they'll repeat it without trying.
- **Tell them what to do with it.** Serve at room temperature. Let it warm twenty minutes. Eat the rind. Pair with a tart apple. Concrete action turns curiosity into experience.

## What to avoid

- Condescension. They've already decided artisanal cheese is worth their time and money. Don't write down to them.
- Empty premium claims (*"crafted with care," "made with passion," "world-class"*) — these don't justify the price; they signal that nothing else does.
- A barrage of dates, breeds, and terms with no story spine. Variable sophistication means some readers will tune out before the second sentence if it reads like a textbook.

## Example pattern

> This is our washed-rind cheese — a cheese where we rub the rind with brine for a few weeks while it ages, which is what builds the savory aroma. The maker washes it just long enough to develop that depth, but the inside stays buttery and gentle, more buttered toast than barnyard. Let it warm up before you eat it. The smell arrives first; the sweetness follows. People who think they don't like washed-rind cheese tend to change their minds with this one.
