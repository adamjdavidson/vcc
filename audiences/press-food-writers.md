# Press and Food Writers

**Purpose:** Food writers want a *story worth telling*, not a product to sell. Specific people, real tension, verifiable detail, access.

## Who they are

A food writer or cheese journalist is looking for **a story worth telling**, not a product to promote. Their question is not "is this cheese good?" but "is there a piece here?" That means they want characters, change, stakes, specificity, and tension — the same things any narrative needs. They have professional skepticism about producer pitches and a low tolerance for marketing language.

The good ones know the cheese landscape well, take real notes, and follow up to verify. The story has to be true, and it has to be checkable.

## What they want from a story

- **Specific people doing specific things.** A maker named, a place named, a choice made, a turning point identified. Generalities don't generate paragraphs.
- **Tension or stakes.** What was the problem? What was at risk? Why does this cheese exist? Without a reason for the cheese to be, there's no story to write.
- **A reason this story matters beyond one product.** Why does this cheese tell us something about Vermont, about dairy economics, about a tradition, about a generation of makers? Writers think in themes.
- **Verifiable facts.** Names, dates, places, numbers a reporter can cross-check. Vague claims raise red flags.
- **Sensory detail done well.** Writers love specific sensory language — the kind that feels reported, not invented.
- **Access.** A producer willing to be on the phone, host a visit, share a milking, or walk through a wash room is much easier to write about than one who hands over a press release.

## How to write for them

- **Lead with a person, a moment, or a problem.** Not the product.
- **Be specific.** Place, year, breed, milk source, named relationships, named mentors. Verifiable detail builds trust.
- **Acknowledge complication.** Stories with only one note read as PR. Honest tension — what was hard, what almost didn't happen, what changed — is what makes a piece publishable.
- **Offer access.** A visit, a phone interview, a sample, a connection to other producers in the same arc.
- **Skip the accolade list.** Awards and ratings are background. They are not the story.

## What to avoid

- Press-release voice ("award-winning," "premium," "world-class," "crafted with passion"). Writers will quote it sarcastically.
- Producer biographies that are flattering but generic.
- Invented drama. Writers fact-check.
- Pitching the cheese as "the best" anything. Pitch the *story*.

## Story test

Ask: *what would make a writer want to call this maker and learn more?*

If the answer is only "the cheese is good," the story is not ready. A good cheese isn't a story; a good cheese plus a particular maker plus a particular pressure plus a particular place — that's a story.
