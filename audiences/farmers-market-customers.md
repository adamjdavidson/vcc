# Farmers Market Customers

**Purpose:** Consumers in face-to-face context. Ten seconds of attention. Speakable language only — read aloud as a test.

## Who they are

A farmers market customer is a general consumer in a specific posture: **standing in front of you with about ten seconds of attention** before they decide whether to taste, ask a question, or move on. They have already opted in to artisanal pricing — they're at a farmers market — but they need an immediate reason to engage with this particular cheese, this particular table.

Their sophistication varies. The same Saturday will bring people who have never tasted a goat cheese and people who can name the maker.

## What they want from a story

- **An invitation.** *"Try this one"* opens the door before any explanation.
- **A speakable name and category.** What is it, in two words a normal person uses? "Our washed-rind tomme" — yes. "An artisan farmstead specialty" — no.
- **One memorable thing.** A single specific that justifies the price and gives them something to tell a friend. Not a list of features. One vivid detail.
- **A sensory cue for the taste.** "Notice how it gets sweeter as it warms" gives them a job and makes the cheese taste better.
- **A use suggestion.** What to do with it tonight. Cheeseboard, pasta, breakfast egg, simple snack. Concrete action turns a sample into a sale.

## How to write for them

- **Speakable, not written.** Read it aloud. If it sounds like brochure copy, rewrite. If it sounds like the cheesemaker actually talking, keep it.
- **Short.** Five lines or fewer for the table pitch. Anything longer loses them.
- **Use "you."** Direct address. Not "consumers will appreciate."
- **Lead with the invitation, not the explanation.** Get a sample in their mouth before you explain anything.

## A useful structure

1. Try this one.
2. It's our [plain category].
3. What makes it different is [specific story detail].
4. Notice [sensory cue] when you taste it.
5. It's good with [pairing or use].

## What to avoid

- Brochure language at a market table.
- Talking longer than the customer will listen.
- Explaining before sampling. The cheese is the strongest argument.

## Example pattern

> Try this one. It's our washed-rind tomme — a cheese where we rub the rind with brine for a few weeks while it ages. Notice how the smell is stronger than the flavor. It's gentler than it looks. People love it on a board with apples or hard cider.
