# Multi-Cheese Makers

**Purpose:** What to do when the cheesemaker has a lineup of cheeses, not just one. The kit's worksheet is built around a single cheese; many real Vermont producers make three to seven.

## The branching question

Before drafting anything, ask the cheesemaker:

> Are you working on the story of one specific cheese in your lineup, or do you want help with the producer story that ties the whole lineup together?

Their answer determines everything else. The two paths are different.

## Path A: One cheese in a lineup

If the cheesemaker wants help with one specific cheese, treat the workshop exactly as the kit normally does. The fact that other cheeses exist is context, not the subject.

The other cheeses become useful raw material in two ways:

1. **Comparison.** *"Of the seven cheeses we make, this is the only one that…"* — comparison to the rest of the lineup is often the cleanest way to surface what's distinctive about a single cheese.
2. **Range as evidence.** *"We make a goat tomme, a bloomy goat, a hard cow's-milk wheel, and a washed-rind. They all come from animals on the same hillside."* — the lineup shows the range of what the maker can do, which can support a single-cheese story.

Don't drift into the producer story by accident. Stay focused on the named cheese.

## Path B: The producer story across the lineup

If the cheesemaker wants the umbrella story — the thing that ties the whole lineup together — the workflow shifts. Ask:

1. **What do all these cheeses share?** Same farm? Same milk source? Same animals? Same maker hands? Same aging cellar? Same philosophy or approach? Same season?
2. **Why do you make this many cheeses?** Practical (to use seasonal milk year-round)? Philosophical (curiosity, range, exploring a tradition)? Commercial (different price points or accounts)? Constraint (the herd produces variable milk that wants different recipes)?
3. **Which cheese is the flagship — the one that introduces the producer to a new customer?** That cheese's story often anchors the lineup story.
4. **Are there cheeses that don't fit?** Honest answer: *"Three of these cheeses share a story; the other two are something else."* Don't force a unified narrative across cheeses that don't share one.

## Producer-level story structure

A producer-level story has its own shape, distinct from a single-cheese story:

1. **The maker (or makers).** Who is doing this work, and what brought them here? Pull from the maker worksheet.
2. **The place.** Where the operation is and what about that place is shaping the work. Pull from the place worksheet and `vermont-context/`.
3. **The herd or milk source.** What animals or what farm partners are providing the raw material across all the cheeses?
4. **The philosophy.** The connecting thread — why this maker makes this range of cheeses in this way. Often the most important sentence in the producer-level story.
5. **The cheeses as expressions.** A line or two on each cheese, framed as different answers to the same underlying question.

Then the individual cheeses can be developed as sub-stories that lean on the producer-level frame.

## What to watch out for

- **Feature listing as story.** *"We make a goat tomme, a bloomy, an aged blue, and a fresh chèvre"* is a list, not a story. The lineup story has to explain *why* these cheeses, not just enumerate them.
- **Generic farm-romance umbrella copy.** *"At Maplewood Farm we make a range of artisan cheeses with passion and care"* could describe any farm. The producer-level story needs the same anti-slop discipline as a single-cheese story.
- **Forcing unity that isn't there.** Some lineups don't share a story — they share a producer, but they came from different impulses, different seasons, or different commercial needs. Honest acknowledgment is better than fake coherence.
- **The recommendation in a single workshop session.** A 9 a.m. workshop probably can't develop seven full cheese stories. Recommend: pick one cheese to anchor the work, draft the producer-level frame, and treat the other cheeses as next-session material.

## Output formats for producer-level stories

- **Website *About* page** — the natural home for the producer-level story. Use the website-copy template's longer form.
- **Producer info sheet** — for buyer outreach, distributor catalogs, press kits. Different from a per-cheese product sheet.
- **Trade-press pitch** — when a writer wants to cover *the producer*, not a specific cheese.
- **Founder remarks at events** — spoken version of the producer-level story.

## Useful follow-up questions

- Among your cheeses, which one would you want a customer to taste first?
- Is there a cheese in your lineup that surprises people? Why?
- Are any of these cheeses seasonal or made only in certain conditions?
- Did any of these cheeses come from a specific moment, problem, or experiment that the others didn't?
- If you had to drop one cheese, which would it be — and why does the rest of the lineup hold without it?
