# How to Use a Worksheet

**Purpose:** How to interpret each of the three printed worksheet types (Cheese, Maker, Place).

The worksheet is a raw-material intake form. It is not a finished story.

The printed packet (`cheese_worksheets.pdf`) is titled *Tell Your Story* and is divided into:

1. **Cover page** — three usage modes (fill out by hand, have Claude interview, voice memo) plus the GitHub URL.
2. **About Your Business** — three checkbox questions about audience, purchase channel, and the asset the cheesemaker wants. **Read this page first when present.** Interpretation is in `workflow/intake-page-interpretation.md`.
3. **Worksheet 1: The Cheese Story** — 9 questions: cheese name; plain description; animal milk and milk/animal details; cheese family/tradition; comparable cheeses; how the maker makes it differently; what physically happens to make or age it; what the maker says when handing someone a sample; what people say when they taste it.
4. **Worksheet 2: The Maker Story** — 6 questions: name and company; what the maker did before cheese; how cheese pulled them in; what was hard about getting here; who taught/influenced/helped/challenged them; what they're proudest of.
5. **Worksheet 3: The Place Story** — 6 questions: where the cheese is made; where it is aged; where the milk comes from; what animals provide the milk; who else is part of the place; what someone would notice if they visited.

A cheesemaker may complete any combination of these — including just one worksheet, or none (in which case use `workflow/interview-from-scratch.md`).

If the cheesemaker has multiple cheeses in their lineup and wants help with the producer-level story rather than (or in addition to) one specific cheese, see `workflow/multi-cheese-makers.md`.

They may also answer by voice memo.

## When a worksheet is uploaded

1. Transcribe the worksheet if necessary.
2. Organize the answers by category.
3. Identify concrete facts and phrases that feel alive.
4. Identify vague or generic language.
5. Research relevant cheese style and place context.
6. Suggest three possible story through-lines.
7. Ask five concrete follow-up questions.

## Do not expect complete answers

Some worksheets will be sparse. That is fine.

If the worksheet is thin, do not scold the user. Ask better questions.

## What counts as useful material

Useful raw material can include:

- a phrase the maker says at a tasting table,
- a customer reaction,
- a production detail,
- a place name,
- a comparison to another cheese,
- a family or mentor story,
- a physical detail from the creamery,
- a hard thing the maker survived,
- a detail about animals or milk,
- a question the maker gets all the time.

Do not privilege polished writing. The most useful material is often casual.
