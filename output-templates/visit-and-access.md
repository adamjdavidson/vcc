# Visit and Access

**Purpose:** Reusable language for inviting cheesemongers, buyers, food writers, and chefs to visit the farm — and what to offer when a visit isn't possible. A small reference any other output can pull from.

## Why this matters

Several audiences actively want access to the producer:

- **Cheesemongers** want to come to the farm. The visit is how they deepen their relationship to the cheese, gather material for their own customer storytelling, and build the kind of connection that influences ordering. (See `audiences/cheesemongers.md`.)
- **Food writers** need the visit to write a piece. No visit, no story.
- **Buyers** appreciate a producer they can visit, even if they don't take the trip. The offer signals confidence and transparency.
- **Chefs** sometimes want to visit, especially regional chefs who menu local sourcing.
- **Consumers and members** of agritourism-friendly co-ops sometimes want to visit too.

## How to extend an invitation that doesn't sound performative

Three rules:

1. **Be specific.** Not *"come visit anytime"* — that reads as ceremonial and tells the recipient nothing. Better: *"we host monger visits twice a year"* or *"if you're ever in [region], call ahead and we'll walk you through the wash room"*.
2. **Name what they'll see.** A visit is more compelling when the producer specifies what's on offer — milking, the make room, the cellar, the herd at pasture, a tasting through aged batches. *"You can be there for morning milking"* is more inviting than *"come see the farm."*
3. **Acknowledge constraints.** A working dairy has rhythms. Be honest about when visits work and when they don't. *"Late spring through fall is best — mud season is rough on the dirt road"* is more useful than pretending the door is always open.

## What to offer when a visit isn't possible

Not every producer can host visitors, and not every recipient can travel. Real alternatives:

- A **video walkthrough** of the make room, the cellar, the herd. Phone-shot is fine.
- A **15-minute call** with the cheesemaker — for cheesemongers about to put the cheese on a counter, or for buyer counter staff being trained on it.
- A **detailed product sheet** with the kind of specifics a monger or buyer would normally have to come visit to learn.
- A **sample with handwritten notes** from the cheesemaker — what to look for, what's distinctive about the batch, what's changing this season.
- An **annual tour** or open-house day, if hosting one-off visits doesn't work but a single yearly window does.

## Sample language

**For cheesemongers (in a product sheet, buyer email, or wholesaler note):**

> If you're ever in the Northeast Kingdom, call ahead and we'll walk you through the wash room and pull a few aged batches to taste. We host monger visits in May and October.

**For buyers (in an outreach email):**

> Happy to send a sample. If you'd like a 15-minute call with [maker name] before placing an opening order, we can walk your counter staff through how to talk about the cheese.

**For food writers (when pitched a piece):**

> You're welcome to come up. May through October works best. You can be there for morning milking, walk through the make room, and taste through batches in the cellar. We've also done phone interviews when the schedule doesn't allow a visit.

**For chefs:**

> If you're ever menu-ing this cheese, we'd love to host you and your team. Best in the warm months. We can also do a video walkthrough of the make room and a tasting kit shipped to the restaurant.

**For consumers (on a website or label):**

> The farm is open to visitors on Saturdays in summer. Reservations help us be ready for you. If you can't visit, the [link] page has a video tour.

## What to avoid

- Generic *"come visit anytime"* without specifics.
- Implying every audience type is welcome on the same terms — a press visit and a farmers-market customer visit are very different and should be addressed differently.
- Promising access the producer can't sustain. A failed visit is worse than no visit.

## How to use this file

Pull the relevant sample line into a buyer email, product sheet, website paragraph, or cheesemonger pitch. Adapt to the producer's actual access situation. Confirm specifics with the cheesemaker before promising visits in any final copy.
