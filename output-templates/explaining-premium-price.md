# Explaining the Premium Price

**Purpose:** Practical language for the moment a customer or buyer asks *"why does this cost $30 a pound?"* The Passion Economy frame in `story-principles/` explains why premium pricing is justified in the abstract; this file gives the cheesemaker actual things to say.

## The healthy stance

Most cheesemakers default to one of three weak postures when the price question lands:

1. **Defensive** — *"I know it seems expensive, but…"* This concedes the question's premise. The customer hasn't said it's expensive yet; the producer just told them it is.
2. **Apologetic** — *"It's a lot of work to make, that's why."* True but flat. The customer already assumed labor goes into food; this doesn't help them justify the price.
3. **Bitter** — *"Well, commodity cheese is destroying small farms."* Even when true, the customer didn't ask about the broader economy. They asked about this wheel.

The healthy stance is **specific and unbothered**. The cheese costs what it costs because of these specific facts. The customer chose to engage with artisan cheese already by walking up to the table or the counter. Respect their intelligence. Lead with what's *in* the cheese.

## Three framings that work

### 1. Specific inputs the price reflects

Lead with concrete things the customer can picture: a single herd. Two miles from the creamery. Six months on a wooden board. Washed by hand three times a week. Made only when the cows are on pasture. **The story facts and the price-justification facts are the same facts.** The story-finding work the kit does is also the price-justification work, just spoken with a different emphasis.

> *This cheese is from a single herd of Brown Swiss cows two miles from the creamery. We wash the rind by hand three times a week. It's aged six months on a board. That's why it's $30 a pound — and that's why it tastes the way it does.*

### 2. Reframe the comparison

The customer is mentally comparing this cheese to **other cheese they buy**. Often that's commodity supermarket cheese. Producers can gently relocate the comparison:

- *"It's expensive compared to commodity cheddar — but it's not really the same product. It's closer to a French Comté than to a block of yellow cheddar."*
- *"Commodity cheese is engineered to be the same every time, anywhere. This one is engineered to taste like the herd, the season, and the room it ages in."*

The reframe says: *you're not paying more for the same thing; you're paying for a different category of thing.*

### 3. The cheese pays for the farm

When place and sustainability are real parts of the story, the price connects to what the customer is supporting:

> *A wheel of cheese at $30 a pound is what lets us keep one herd and one creamery running on this hillside. The math is straightforward — commodity milk pricing wouldn't keep this farm alive.*

Use only when the cheesemaker has confirmed that the operation actually depends on premium pricing in this way. Don't borrow the line from the broader artisan-cheese narrative if it's not true of *this* producer.

## Sample language for different audiences

**At a market table or cheese counter (consumer):**

> *This is a small-herd cheese — one farm, one cellar, six months of aging. That's most of why it's priced where it is. Try it before you decide.*

**To a retail buyer:**

> *The price tier sits between standard specialty and high-end imports. Customers who buy real Comté or aged Gruyère will not flinch. The reason it can support this price: it's farmstead, single-herd, hand-washed, and aged on the board. Margin works at this point.*

**To a cheesemonger:**

> *We hold $30 a pound at the producer level. Most of that goes into the milk side — the herd we keep on pasture, the smaller crew we run, the wheel format that takes longer to age out. Customers who are willing to pay for a real farmstead cheddar respond to it.*

**To a chef:**

> *At our wholesale price, this cheese pencils on a $24 cheese plate or as a finishing element on a $42 entrée. The cost reflects the milk side of the operation more than the production side.*

**To a food writer (who is asking implicitly):**

> The cheese costs what it costs because the herd is small, the milk doesn't travel, the aging is hand-managed, and the volume is low. A larger operation could not produce this cheese at this price point. A larger operation could not produce this cheese at all.

## What to avoid

- *"You get what you pay for."* True but lazy. Doesn't tell the customer anything.
- *"It's a labor of love."* Says nothing the customer can taste or remember.
- Detailed cost breakdowns at the point of sale. The customer is asking *why*, not *how much went where*. Save the spreadsheet for buyers who actually need it.
- Comparing favorably to even more expensive cheeses (*"compared to a real Roquefort, it's a steal"*). Sounds defensive.
- Apologizing for the price.

## How to use this file

When a cheesemaker asks for help with how to explain pricing, pull the appropriate framing for their named audience. The price-explanation language draws on the same story facts the kit has already developed — there is no separate story to write. Confirm with the cheesemaker that the specifics in the sample lines (single herd, six months on the board, etc.) are literally true of their cheese before any line is used in finished copy.
