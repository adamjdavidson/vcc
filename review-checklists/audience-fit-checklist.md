# Audience-Fit Checklist

**Purpose:** Does this draft match the named audience's posture and language?

Run this on every draft. The same underlying story should be able to flex for different audiences — but a single draft must clearly serve one named audience well.

## The checks

1. **Name the audience.** Can you state in one sentence who this draft is for? (For example: "co-op cheesemonger who has 20 seconds at the counter.") If not, you have not committed to an audience yet.

2. **Read the relevant audience file.** Before judging fit, look at the file in `audiences/` that corresponds to the named audience. The checks below depend on what that audience needs.

3. **Technical detail level.** Does the draft assume the right amount of cheese vocabulary? A general-consumer draft should not require knowing what *lactic*, *bloomy*, *pasta filata*, or *affineur* means. A cheesemonger draft can.

4. **Length and shape.** Does the draft fit the format the audience actually encounters?
   - Farmers market customer: a short spoken invitation.
   - Cheesemonger: 15–30 seconds of memorable, repeatable language.
   - Retail buyer: practical reasons it belongs in their case, plus staff-useful language.
   - Wholesaler/distributor: a portable capsule that survives being told by someone who has never visited the farm.
   - Chef: flavor, behavior with heat, format, pairing, reliability or seasonal variation.
   - Co-op or independent grocer: producer/place story plus customer-friendly cues plus values fit.
   - Press / food writer: a real narrative — characters, stakes, change, specificity.
   - General consumer: plain language, a reason to care, something to notice.

5. **Repeatability.** If this audience needs to repeat the story to someone else (most do), can a normal person remember and re-tell at least one line of this draft from memory? If not, simplify.

6. **Practical usefulness.** Does the draft help the audience *do something* — buy, sell, serve, recommend, eat, share, write about, remember? If it does not give the audience a next step or a thing to notice, it is not yet useful.

7. **Pandering check.** Did you flatten the truth or lean on stereotypes about the audience? (For example, assuming all chefs care only about pairings, or all buyers care only about margin.) If yes, restore the underlying story.

## What to do with failures

If the draft fails on audience fit, do not paper over it with extra sentences. Either commit harder to the named audience, or split the asset into separate audience versions.
