# Anti-Slop Checklist

**Purpose:** Could this draft describe any cheese? Catch generic AI copy before it ships.

Run this on every draft of finished copy. The goal is to catch language that could describe any cheese, any farm, any maker.

## The checks

1. **The substitution test.** Could you put a different cheesemaker's name and a different cheese style at the top of this copy and have it still read as plausible? If yes, the copy is too generic. Replace the generic claims with specifics from the worksheet.

2. **The empty-adjective test.** Are any of these words doing real work in the copy: *artisan, authentic, premium, unique, local, handcrafted, traditional, small-batch, world-class, exceptional, crafted with care, made with passion*? If they appear without a concrete fact backing them up, cut or replace them.

3. **The pastoral-cliché test.** Does the copy say *nestled in the hills*, *rolling hills of Vermont*, *charming*, *picturesque*, *bucolic*, *quaint*, *timeless*, *farm-fresh goodness*, or *flavor journey*? If yes, replace with a concrete place detail.

4. **The unsupported-claim test.** Is there any sentence asserting something about the cheese, the maker, the milk, the animals, the place, or the process that is not in the worksheet, voice memo, interview, or a clearly cited outside source? If yes, ask the cheesemaker to confirm or cut it.

5. **The checklist-story test.** Does the copy give one sentence about milk, one about animals, one about weather, one about geography, one about the maker, and so on — without showing how those facts explain one another? If yes, the copy is a list, not a story. Find the unifying thread and rewrite around it.

6. **The center-of-gravity test.** Can you say in one sentence what this story is *about* — what its organizing idea is? If you cannot, the draft does not yet have a story.

7. **The repeatability test.** If a cheesemonger or co-op staffer read this draft once, could they walk to a counter and say something useful from memory? If not, the draft is not staff-friendly.

## What to do with failures

A single failure means the draft needs a revision before the cheesemaker sees it. Do not "explain around" the failure in your response — fix the draft.
