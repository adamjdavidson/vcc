# Good Story Patterns

**Purpose:** Six recurring story patterns that work for cheese. Patterns to recognize, not templates to copy.

These are patterns, not templates. Do not force every cheese into one of them.

## Pattern 1: The old solution in a new place

A traditional cheese style developed to solve a problem in one place. A Vermont maker adapts that logic to a new landscape, milk source, and market.

Works well for:

- Alpine-style cheeses,
- cheddar,
- washed-rind cheeses,
- tommes,
- pasta filata,
- any cheese with a clear lineage.

Key question:

> What problem did the original style solve, and what problem or desire does this version answer now?

## Pattern 2: The maker’s impossible choice

A maker faces a constraint — money, land, milk, regulation, climate, labor, market access, aging capacity — and the cheese becomes the solution.

Works well when:

- the maker has a clear turning point,
- the cheese exists because of a problem,
- the business model matters,
- customers need to understand price.

Key question:

> What pressure shaped this cheese?

## Pattern 3: Place as active force

The place is not scenery. The land, weather, milk source, local economy, or aging environment actively shapes the cheese.

Works well when:

- exact location matters,
- milk or forage changes seasonally,
- aging conditions are place-specific,
- local history or landscape directly affects the product.

Key question:

> What does this place do to the cheese or the business?

## Pattern 4: The sensory reversal

The cheese surprises people. It may smell strong but taste gentle, look simple but ripen dramatically, or sound familiar but behave differently.

Works well for:

- washed rinds,
- blues,
- bloomy rinds,
- fresh goat cheeses,
- familiar categories with surprising versions.

Key question:

> What does the audience expect, and what actually happens?

## Pattern 5: The regional-renewal story

A cheese is part of a larger attempt to keep farms, land, skills, or rural economies alive.

This can be powerful, but only if supported by real facts.

Works well when:

- the maker has a clear community mission,
- the creamery supports multiple farms,
- the cheese creates value from milk that might otherwise be undervalued,
- the business model is part of the story.

Key question:

> How does this cheese participate in a larger economic or community effort?

## Pattern 6: The table story

The story centers on the experience of eating and sharing the cheese.

Works well when:

- the cheese has a strong serving ritual,
- the cheese changes as it warms,
- the audience is consumers or event guests,
- the maker has a natural spoken pitch.

Key question:

> What does knowing this story help someone notice while eating?
