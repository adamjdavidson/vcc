# Fictional Sample Stories

**Purpose:** Fictional cheese stories used to teach the unified-narrative pattern. Clearly labeled fiction; not real producers.

These examples are fictional. Do not treat them as facts about real cheesemakers.

## Sample 1: Consumer version

Marsh Road Tomme began as a practical answer to spring milk. The farm’s cows give richer, sweeter milk when pasture finally comes in, but the creamery needed a cheese patient enough to hold that season instead of rushing it to market. So the maker chose a natural-rind tomme: simple in shape, slow in aging, and open to the cellar. The result is a cheese that tastes mild at first, then gets deeper as it warms — hay, butter, mushroom, and the memory of grass.

## Sample 2: Cheesemonger version

Marsh Road Tomme is a natural-rind cow’s milk cheese made to preserve the farm’s spring pasture milk. It sits somewhere between a rustic tomme and a young Alpine-style wheel: mild and buttery at first, then more earthy as it warms. The story to tell customers is simple: this is a cheese built around patience. It turns a short Vermont pasture season into something you can cut and serve months later.

## Sample 3: Buyer-facing version

Marsh Road Tomme gives retailers an accessible Vermont farmstead cheese with a clear story and broad customer appeal. It has enough category familiarity to sit near tommes or young Alpine-style cheeses, but its strongest selling point is the way it captures spring pasture milk in a format that becomes more complex with age. Staff can sell it as mild, buttery, earthy, and easy to pair — with a concise story about turning seasonal milk into lasting value.
