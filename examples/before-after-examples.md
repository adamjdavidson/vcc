# Before and After Examples

**Purpose:** Side-by-side weak-vs-strong cheese copy, with explanation of why each version succeeds or fails.

These are fictional examples designed to teach story improvement.

## Example 1: Washed-rind cheese

### Before

> Our washed-rind cheese is handcrafted in Vermont using traditional methods. It has a unique aroma and creamy flavor.

### Why it fails

The copy names a category but gives no story. It does not explain the washing, the aroma, the maker’s choices, or the customer experience.

### Better

> This is the cheese we use to teach people not to fear the rind. We wash it through the first weeks of aging until the surface turns savory and alive, but the paste stays gentle — more buttered toast than barnyard. At the table, let it warm before judging it. The smell arrives first; the sweetness follows.

### Why it works

- It has a situation: people fear washed rinds.
- It has a maker action: washing during aging.
- It has sensory movement: aroma first, sweetness later.
- It gives the audience something to do.

## Example 2: Cheddar

### Before

> A sharp Vermont cheddar made with high-quality local milk.

### Better

> This cheddar is our argument against the idea that cheddar is only a block in a grocery case. We make it slowly, press it hard, and age it until the paste starts to break into a clean, savory crumble. It is familiar enough for anyone to recognize, but patient enough to remind you that cheddar was once a farm cheese before it became a commodity.

### Why it works

- It names the commodity trap.
- It restores cheddar’s history and texture.
- It connects method to meaning.
- It gives a buyer or cheesemonger a repeatable line.

## Example 3: Place

### Before

> Our cheese is made in beautiful rural Vermont.

### Better

> The cheese is made in a town where winter still organizes the year. Milk changes when pasture disappears, aging rooms behave differently when the air dries out, and the maker has to adjust by feel. The story is not simply that the cheese is from Vermont; it is that the cheese comes from a place where the seasons keep interrupting the recipe.

### Why it works

- It makes place active, not decorative.
- It connects weather and season to craft.
- It suggests a story through-line.
- It still needs confirmation from the maker before use.
